// GDD 211 · Spring 2026 · Team Project

Mafia Deli:
Leave the Gun,
Take the Sandwich

A Doom-style FPS set in Tony's Deli — a mafia front sandwiched between bullet casings and Italian subs. Run the deli, build sandwiches, and survive when rival families crash the shop.

EngineUnity
CourseGDD 211
Team Size3 people
My RoleArt Director
PlatformPC (Windows)
▶ Play Now itch.io

// overview

The Concept

The project started with a single question: "What if a mafia front actually had good business?" From there, the team — myself, Lucas Lettie (writer & director), and Hayden (lead programmer) — built a game that mashes retro FPS combat with point-and-click sandwich building. You run Tony's Deli on the surface, but rival families have other ideas.

The game draws inspiration from Papa's Pizzeria for its food-themed charm, Doom for the retro FPS feel, and a distinctly comedic Italian-American visual identity throughout. All art assets were built from scratch by the team.

Final interior — dining room looking toward the back counter
Final portrait wall — mafia-themed framed photos sourced and edited by Phill

// contribution 01 · ~18 hours

Indoor Environment

Building the Deli Interior

~18 hrs

The interior was built using Unity's ProBuilder tool to construct the architectural shell — walls, partitions, the front counter divide, and room layout — following a floor plan I drafted beforehand. Textures were sourced as free assets and applied as Unity Materials, including normal maps and height maps for surface depth.

I trialled textures side-by-side using two test structures before committing to the final combination. Once the shell was complete, assets were sourced and placed throughout:

  • Retro diner booth seating, tables, ceiling fans, kitchen equipment — Unity Asset Store
  • Caution tape, cardboard boxes, dirty toilet — imported for back-of-house atmosphere
  • Food props placed on counters and dining surfaces

// contribution 03 · ~7 hours

Outdoor Environment

Building the Exterior World

~7 hrs

The outdoor environment establishes the world beyond Tony's Deli — a cracked asphalt lot enclosed by chain-link fencing, with a layered parallax background of a city skyline and suburban treelines stretching into the distance.

  • Created cityscape and residential silhouette PNGs in Canva; aligned as parallax layers in Unity
  • Added a green fill sprite below the horizon to prevent sky bleeding through background gaps
  • Imported chain-link fence, dirty dumpster (with custom mesh collider to prevent clipping), trees, and procedural skybox from Unity Asset Store
  • Imported free ground textures (grass, sidewalk, asphalt); duplicated and modified the asphalt texture to create a road surface variant; painted terrain maps across the exterior space
  • Custom-modelled and textured the Tony's Deli neon sign — my own asset — brought into Unity with a custom flickering Shader Graph

// contribution 05 · ~3 hours

Enemy Sprites

Creating 6 Enemy Character Sprites

~3 hrs

Created 6 total enemy sprites — 3 melee knife-wielding types and 3 ranged gun types — all maintaining a consistent grimy crime aesthetic while having visually distinct silhouettes and outfits. Source images were processed through Photopea to remove backgrounds and crop, then pixelated with Canva's pixelator to match the game's retro Doom style. Sprites were imported into Unity and placed on the NavMesh as flat billboard characters that rotate to face the player.

// overview

The Mission

Five days ago, radio and surveillance connections were lost with the USS Centurion, a state-of-the-art satellite station. You're sent on a recon mission — equipped with a camera, a flashlight, and a journal to log any issues you find. A cosmic storm is closing in. For all intents and purposes, you'll be on your own.

The game is a tense, atmospheric exploration experience. Co-created with Lucas Lettie, with myself handling the environment design and level layout throughout development.

// contribution · environment design

Building the Space Station

Greyboxing & Level Layout

I was responsible for building and designing the environment for the game — drafting up a greyboxed version of the space station until we landed on a layout everyone was happy with. The interconnected floorplan of the station took the most iteration; getting the room relationships right was critical to the game's sense of exploration and tension.

Once the greybox was approved, the real challenge began: taking the unscaled whitebox geometry and building out proper interior detail — walls, corners, ceiling trims, and floor panels — while reconciling the scale mismatch between the greybox and the imported interior structure models.

Finally, I dressed the space with props, clutter, and furniture — both organised and disorganised — to make the station feel lived-in, as if people were once there but are no longer.

An Art-First Vertical Slice

Horus: Vengeance Reborn was a solo final project built around art direction above all else. The gameplay is intentionally minimal — a functional vertical slice that gives context to the visual world — but the art carries the experience. The project experiments with mechanics that blend mythological storytelling and interactive design, featuring dynamic traversal across desert landscapes and combat encounters inspired by ancient lore. The result bridges history and fantasy through a strong, consistent visual identity.

Simple Mechanics, Art-Driven Execution

Factory Flip was a GDD 200 final project designed as a proof-of-concept: could I build a fully functional, playable game with a single button input? The answer is yes — but more importantly, it's a demonstration that even a mechanically minimal game benefits enormously from strong visual direction.

The code handles the core loop — a conveyor belt, crate sorting, scoring — but the art gives it personality. The factory setting, visual feedback, and overall aesthetic were all deliberate choices to make a one-button game feel like it has a world behind it.

// GDD 202 · Spring 2026 · Solo Project

It's A Small World

A tinkerer's workbench — but the tinkerer is a bug. A full 3D art pipeline project covering reference gathering, moodboarding, modelling, UV unwrapping, PBR texturing, shader work, lighting, and final scene presentation in Unity.

CourseGDD 202
TypeSolo
ToolsMaya · Substance · Unity
DeliverableFull Pipeline Scene

// concept

The Idea

The assignment brief called for a "Slice of Life" 3D environment scene — a small, atmospheric location that feels specific, lived-in, and visually intentional. I wanted to create a sort of "tinkerer's workbench" style of scene, but with a twist: the tinkerer is a bug.

From that concept I settled on building tools and small creations with bug-inspired modifications — scales, cartilage, discoloration, and organic textures that make familiar objects feel alien and intentional. The mood is warm, cluttered, and slightly otherworldly.

Project concept — tinkerer's workbench with a bug as the craftsman
Moodboard — workbench references, bug anatomy, lighting inspiration, and character direction

// step 01 · modelling

4 Original Assets in Maya

Hero Asset + 3 Supporting Assets

Four original assets were modelled from scratch in Maya, each serving a specific role in the scene hierarchy:

  • Lantern Bug Lamp — hero asset, the focal point of the scene
  • Mandible Wrench — background asset, bug-jaw-inspired hand tool
  • Battery Core — background asset, organic-meets-mechanical power cell
  • Workbench — world asset, grounds the scene and provides context
MayaHard SurfaceOrganic FormsAsset Planning
All 4 assets modelled in Maya — hero lamp (1), workbench (2), mandible wrench (3), battery core (4)

// step 02 · UV unwrapping & texturing

UV Maps & Substance Painter

Per-Component UV Unwrap → PBR Texturing

Each component of the hero asset was UV unwrapped separately in Maya, with clean seam placement and minimal distortion. The full set was then textured in Substance Painter using a PBR Metal-Roughness workflow — layering rust patterns, AO bakes, scale textures, and organic surface detail.

One notable technique: the lamp bulb texture uses Substance Painter's PBR Metal-Roughness Blending with Alpha to achieve a translucent glass appearance — rather than a standard opaque surface.

UV MappingSubstance PainterPBR PipelineAlpha BlendingAO Baking
Hero asset UV layout — per-part unwrap across all lamp components
Hero asset texture maps — translucent bulb, wing scales, arm rust, cage patina

// step 03 · unity integration & shaders

Engine Import & Custom Shader Graphs

3 Shader Graphs — Standard, Wobble, Transparent Emission

All four assets were imported into Unity with their full PBR material sets. Three custom Shader Graphs were created to bring the scene to life beyond standard materials:

  • Custom Standard Shader — base shader modified to receive the Substance texture maps correctly
  • Wobbling Distortion Shader — vertex displacement shader creating an organic wobble effect on the Battery Core, driven by time-based noise
  • Transparent + Emission Variant — variation of the wobble graph adding transparency and emissive glow for the lamp bulb

// step 04 · lighting & final render

Scene Assembly & Final Render

Dressed Scene with Lighting & Supporting Assets

The final scene was assembled in Unity with point lighting sourced from the lamp itself as the key light, supplemented by supporting prop assets to fill the workbench surface. The cave-wall background grounds the scene spatially and adds depth. All lighting, asset placement, and scene composition were designed to reinforce the warm, secretive atmosphere of a bug's hidden workshop.
